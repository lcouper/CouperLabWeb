Our research scope

Global change is transforming the landscape of infectious diseases, from the poleward expansion of vector-borne disease to the spillover of novel human pathogens from animal reservoirs. Our group studies the ecological and evolutionary responses of infectious disease systems to global change, with a primary focus on vector-borne and environmentally-mediated pathogens. We use a combination of lab experiments, field surveys, and genomics. By disentangling linkages between global change and infectious disease, our overarching aim is to advance strategies to protect both human and planetary health.

Quantifying impacts
The transmission of vector-borne and zoonotic diseases is fundamentally linked to environmental conditions. Yet isolating the impact of specific environmental drivers as infectious diseaes exist across complex, dynamic socio-ecological settings. We leverage analytical approaches for causal inference in complex systems, often borrowing approaches from econometrics and epidemiology, to isolate and quantify the impact of specific global change drivers of transmission. Recent examples include warming and Lyme disease in the US and oil and gas development and Valley fever in California.

Emergence
Aided by rapid global changes, diseaes such as dengue and Valley fever have emerged in recent decades.

Dispersal and invasion
Aedes aegypti and Ae. albopictus are two of the most globally important invasive mosquito vectors that transmit dengue, Zika, and other viruses. These species have rapidly expanded within the U.S. in recent decades, including recent detections of Ae. albopictus in Michigan. However, the processes underlying their dispersal and expansion are poorly understood, as the putative drivers—climate, land use change, and human movement—occur over vastly different temporal and spatial scales.

Eco-evolutionary responses
Climate change is poised to cause massive changes in vector-borne disease distributions, with predicted expansions at cool-range edges and contractions at warm-range edges. However, the potential for vectors to adapt to warming--and therefore persist at warm edges--remains largely unknown as their evolutionary potential is poorly understood. We use field surveys, experimentaiton, and genomic analysis to investigate adaptive potential. In recent work, we found evidence of current thermal adaptation in mosquitoes, potential for adaptive evolution on pace with warming, and modeled impacts of adaptation on disease incidence.
