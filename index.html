Our research

Global change is transforming the landscape of infectious diseases, from the poleward expansion of vector-borne disease to the spillover of novel human pathogens from animal reservoirs. Yet predicting these shifts is challenging as infectious disease systems are embedded in diverse ecological communities and are dynamically responding to multiple concurrent changes. In this talk, I will address the ecological and evolutionary responses of infectious disease systems to global change, with a primary focus on vector-borne and environmentally-mediated pathogens. Using a combination of lab experiments, field surveys, and genomics, we have found that mosquitoes have the potential to rapidly adapt to warming, potentially averting predicted declines at warm range edges. At the same time, we have found that warming at cold range edges is facilitating mosquito-borne disease emergence, which I will illustrate through our work on locally acquired dengue in the continental U.S. I will conclude by describing the emergence of human fungal pathogens, and our ongoing work combining environmental and clinical isolates of Coccidioides—the causative agent of Valley fever—to understand how and where humans come to be infected. Ultimately, my aim is to disentangle linkages between global change and infectious disease, with the goal of advancing strategies to protect both human and planetary health.

My work aims to identify the potential for adaptive evolution in infectious disease systems, drawing on concepts and approaches from conservation biology, evolutionary genomics, and disease ecology.
